Hyper Text Transfer Protocol

Hyper Text Transfer Protocol or HTTP for short, is the language computers use to ask another computer for a document. When you open your web browser and enter a URL, your computer communicates with a server and asks a DNS (domain name server) for the binary version of the URL.

An example the HTTP in action is when you try navigate to a website, your computer submits a GET request for the website's HTML so your computer knows what to show you in your web browser. Some pages with many images may take a longer time to load as the images load in on separate HTTP requests.

When your computer receives the SUCCESS of loading a web page, a cookie is saved in your browser which is a unique identifier to help the web server know who you are and adjust the browsing experience accordingly.

HTTP requests without security would be completely vulnerable to hackers. Two security protocols, secure sockets layer and transport layer security, ensure your HTTP requests are protected. They act as a layer of security around your data. When your website asks your browser for a secure connection, your browser acts for a digital certificate which are published by certificate authorities that are trusted entities that verify identities of websites.
